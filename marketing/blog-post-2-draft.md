## Full Draft

### Introduction

If you've ever had a fasting blood glucose of 92 — technically in the "normal" range — and been told you're fine, you may not actually be fine.

Here's why: fasting glucose tells you what your blood sugar is doing right now. What it doesn't tell you is how hard your pancreas is working to keep it there.

That's where HOMA-IR comes in.

HOMA-IR — Homeostatic Model Assessment of Insulin Resistance — is a simple calculation using two measurements you can get from a standard blood draw. It's the best widely available estimate of insulin resistance, and it can show you problems that A1c and fasting glucose alone will miss — sometimes by years.

For South Asian men specifically, HOMA-IR may be the single most important metabolic marker you're not tracking. Here's why.

---

### What Is HOMA-IR?

HOMA-IR is calculated from two values, both drawn during a fasting blood test:

**HOMA-IR = (Fasting Insulin × Fasting Glucose) ÷ 405**

*(When glucose is measured in mg/dL, which is the standard in the US)*

If your fasting insulin is 12 μU/mL and your fasting glucose is 95 mg/dL:

HOMA-IR = (12 × 95) ÷ 405 = **2.81**

The formula was developed in the 1980s by researchers at Oxford and has been validated extensively in population studies since. It's not exotic. It's not controversial. It's just rarely ordered in routine primary care.

---

### What Do the Numbers Mean?

**General reference ranges:**

| HOMA-IR | Interpretation |
|---------|---------------|
| <1.0 | Excellent insulin sensitivity |
| 1.0–1.9 | Normal range for healthy adults |
| 2.0–2.9 | Early insulin resistance — worth monitoring |
| 3.0–4.9 | Significant insulin resistance — intervention indicated |
| ≥5.0 | Severe insulin resistance — high metabolic and cardiovascular risk |

**Important caveat:** These ranges are population averages. There's active research suggesting that South Asians may have clinically significant insulin resistance at HOMA-IR values that would appear "normal" in general population data. A score of 2.5 in a South Asian man with a family history of diabetes and a waist circumference above 90cm tells a different story than the same score in a lean, active patient with no family history.

Context always matters. The number is a starting point, not a verdict.

---

### Why South Asian Men Are at Particular Risk

South Asian men develop insulin resistance at lower body weights and earlier ages than most other populations. The reasons are incompletely understood but well-documented:

**1. The thin-fat phenotype**  
South Asians tend to store fat disproportionately in the visceral (abdominal) compartment even at normal BMIs. Visceral fat is metabolically active — it drives insulin resistance, inflammation, and cardiovascular risk at levels that subcutaneous fat does not.

A South Asian man at a BMI of 24 may have a similar visceral fat burden to a white man at a BMI of 30. Standard BMI-based screening will miss the SA man.

**2. Lower lean muscle mass**  
Skeletal muscle is the primary consumer of glucose during insulin-mediated uptake. South Asians, on average, have less muscle mass relative to fat mass than Western populations at comparable BMIs. Less muscle = less glucose clearance = higher insulin demand for the same level of blood sugar control.

**3. Faster progression from prediabetes**  
In the MASALA cohort study — the largest longitudinal study of South Asian cardiovascular health in the United States — SA adults progressed from prediabetes to diabetes more rapidly than comparison populations. Catching insulin resistance early has higher stakes for us than for many other groups.

**4. Underdiagnosis due to population-derived reference ranges**  
HOMA-IR reference ranges published in medical literature are largely derived from European and American cohort studies. A score of 2.0 may represent low-normal for a white American and early-significant for a South Asian man with the same body composition and family history. The cutoff isn't wrong — it's just not calibrated for us.

---

### Why Your Doctor Isn't Testing It

Most primary care physicians don't order fasting insulin — and therefore can't calculate HOMA-IR — for a few reasons:

1. **It's not in standard guidelines.** Current ADA and USPSTF recommendations for diabetes screening center on A1c, fasting glucose, or oral glucose tolerance test. Fasting insulin is not a standard part of the workup.

2. **Insurance coverage is inconsistent.** Fasting insulin is not always covered for asymptomatic patients, which creates friction in a busy practice.

3. **Most electronic health records don't automatically calculate HOMA-IR.** It requires a manual calculation or an add-on tool.

4. **The clinical significance of "borderline" HOMA-IR values isn't taught widely.** Without subspecialty training in metabolic medicine, many primary care physicians don't have a clear protocol for what to do with a HOMA-IR of 2.8.

None of this is a criticism of your primary care doctor. It's a gap between what routine care is designed to deliver and what the evidence says patients like you actually need.

---

### How to Get Your HOMA-IR

**Step 1:** Ask your doctor to add **fasting insulin** to your next blood draw. You likely already get fasting glucose drawn with your annual metabolic panel — fasting insulin is drawn at the same time, from the same blood sample. It's one additional tube.

The CPT code for fasting insulin is **83525**. If your doctor's office isn't familiar with it, that's the reference.

**Step 2:** Calculate your HOMA-IR using the formula above, or ask your doctor to calculate it for you:

> **(Fasting Insulin [μU/mL] × Fasting Glucose [mg/dL]) ÷ 405**

**Step 3:** Interpret the result in context — age, BMI, waist circumference, family history, and your other metabolic markers.

---

### What to Do If Your HOMA-IR Is Elevated

A HOMA-IR above 2.0 — particularly in a South Asian man with visceral adiposity or family history — is a signal worth acting on. It means your cells are not responding efficiently to insulin, and your pancreas is compensating by producing more.

The window for intervention matters. In the years before A1c rises into the prediabetes range, insulin resistance is often reversible with:

**Lifestyle changes with the strongest evidence:**
- **Resistance training** — the single most effective modality for improving insulin sensitivity. 2–3 sessions per week of compound movements (not just cardio).
- **Reducing refined carbohydrate load** — specifically the glycemic index of carbohydrate sources, not necessarily the total quantity. Swapping white rice for basmati with cooling (which increases resistant starch), reducing portion sizes of roti, adding more dal/legumes to balance the plate.
- **Time-restricted eating** — 16:8 or 14:10 eating windows have shown HOMA-IR reduction in several RCTs, likely through improving insulin sensitivity during the overnight fasting period.
- **Sleep quality** — poor sleep acutely worsens insulin resistance. If you're sleeping under 6 hours or have untreated obstructive sleep apnea (common in SA men), this is a metabolic risk factor independent of diet.
- **Weight loss** — specifically visceral fat loss; even a 5–10% reduction in body weight can significantly improve HOMA-IR.

**Medication, when indicated:**
- Metformin is FDA-approved for prediabetes prevention and has a strong evidence base for reducing HOMA-IR
- GLP-1 receptor agonists (Ozempic, Mounjaro) show dramatic reductions in insulin resistance alongside weight loss — increasingly used in high-risk prediabetes before A1c reaches the diabetes threshold
- Neither should be initiated without a physician evaluation of your full metabolic picture

---

### When to See a Specialist

If your HOMA-IR is above 2.5 and you're South Asian with any of the following, I'd recommend a full metabolic evaluation:
- Waist circumference > 90cm
- A1c > 5.4%
- First-degree relative (parent or sibling) with type 2 diabetes
- Triglycerides > 150 mg/dL
- HDL < 40 mg/dL

These are cumulative risk factors that, in combination with elevated HOMA-IR, significantly increase your 10-year risk of type 2 diabetes — even if every individual marker looks "borderline" or "slightly high."

---

### The Bottom Line

HOMA-IR is a window into your metabolic health that A1c and fasting glucose don't fully open. For South Asian men — who face insulin resistance earlier, at lower BMIs, and with faster disease progression than standard guidelines assume — knowing this number can mean catching a problem years before it becomes irreversible.

Ask for fasting insulin at your next annual blood draw. Calculate HOMA-IR. Know your number.
